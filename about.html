Arthur Raines

Policy researcher and data analyst dedicated to understanding how economic forces shape opportunities for vulnerable populations.

Where I Began

I was born in a small village in Liberia, toward the end of the Second Liberian Civil War. To a young child, life there seemed almost impossibly peaceful. Each morning, I'd bathe with a giant oval-shaped bowl, then wander into the forest to a mucky stream where I'd spend hours catching minnows with my bare hands.

After playing in the forest, I'd return to the village for the evening meal. One day, the men came back carrying an enormous python. The women set to work immediately, cutting it up for a stew, preparing a large pot with spices I couldn't name. When the signal came to eat, I didn't hesitate.

Those tranquil, carefree days ended the way such days often do in places touched by war: suddenly, and without warning.

My memories of what came next are hazy. I remember being moved, often, from one location to another. The reasons were never explained to me, or if they were, I was too young to understand. Once, armed men were spotted nearby. One walked just a few feet from where I was hiding.

The world froze. The only sound was my heart pounding in my chest. My body felt pinned down by some invisible weight. I had never known fear like that. It wasn't until a guardian whispered that we were safe that the weight finally lifted.

Several years later, at seven years old, I was adopted and moved from Liberia, the only place I had ever known, to a suburban household outside Chicago. The contrast was jarring. I found myself in a completely alien society, trying to make sense of a world that operated by entirely different rules.

My Journey

These memories stay with me: the uncertainty, the fear, the disorientation of starting over. They are why I do the work I do today and why I chose to study economics. Not economics in the abstract, but economics as a way of understanding how the world fails people, and how it might fail them less.

At Dartmouth, I began exploring these questions directly. I examined the impact of violence on educational attainment for children in Nigeria. The project was eye-opening, not just for what I found, but for what it revealed about the tools economists have to measure harm, and the limits of those tools. It's where my interest in microeconomics took root.

For my honors thesis, I turned to Tanzania and a puzzle that seemed, at first glance, counterintuitive: what happens to school enrollment when gold prices rise? Using three rounds of the Tanzanian National Panel Survey and a difference-in-differences strategy, I found that higher gold prices led to lower enrollment among children living near artisanal mines. Families got wealthier, but kids left school. The opportunity cost of education, it turns out, rises with the price of gold.

After graduation, I wanted to understand the other side of the equation: how capital markets shape the choices available to families and communities. As a municipal bonds analyst at Nuveen, I focused on general obligation bonds, charter school bonds, and multi-family housing projects. What I learned was sobering. Market forces don't just respond to inequities. They can perpetuate them.

Now, as a Research Analyst at the Early Childhood Research Alliance of Chicago (EC*REACH), I work with city agencies in Chicago to clean, organize, and analyze administrative data on early childhood education. I led a project to unduplicate children across seven administrative databases using probabilistic matching techniques. I've contributed to several papers evaluating Chicago's Universal Pre-K program. The work is technical, but the stakes are not. Behind every record is a child, and behind every policy decision is the question of whether we're giving that child a fair shot.
