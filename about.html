Arthur Raines

Researcher and Entrepreneur dedicated to understanding how economic forces shape opportunities for vulnerable populations.

Where I Began

I was born in a small village in Liberia, toward the end of the Second Liberian Civil War. To a young child, life there seemed almost impossibly peaceful. Each morning, I'd bathe with a giant oval-shaped bowl, then wander into the forest to a mucky stream where I'd spend hours catching minnows with my bare hands.

After playing in the forest, I'd return to the village for the evening meal. One day, the men came back carrying an enormous python. The women set to work immediately, cutting it up for a stew, preparing a large pot with spices I couldn't name.

My path from Liberia to the United States was neither straight nor simple.

I arrived in the U.S. as a young child, settling first with family in New Jersey before eventually making my way to the Midwest. The transition was jarring in ways that are hard to articulate. Language, culture, climate - everything was different. But what stayed constant was a deep curiosity about how the world works, and a growing awareness that the circumstances of your birth shape your life in ways most people never fully reckon with.

The question that drives my work is deceptively simple: why do some children get a fair shot and others don't?

At Dartmouth, I studied Economics and Geography, drawn to the intersection of quantitative rigor and real-world complexity. My undergraduate research took me in two directions that would define my career. First, I examined the impact of violence on educational attainment for children in Nigeria. The project was eye-opening, not just for what I found, but for what it revealed about the tools economists have to measure harm, and the limits of those tools. It's where my interest in microeconomics took root.

For my honors thesis, I turned to Tanzania and a puzzle that seemed, at first glance, counterintuitive: what happens to school enrollment when gold prices rise? Using three rounds of the Tanzanian National Panel Survey and a difference-in-differences strategy, I found that higher gold prices led to lower enrollment among children living near artisanal mines. Families got wealthier, but kids left school. The opportunity cost of education, it turns out, rises with the price of gold.

After graduation, I wanted to understand the other side of the equation: how capital markets shape the choices available to families and communities. As a municipal bonds analyst at Nuveen, I focused on general obligation bonds, charter school bonds, and multi-family housing projects. What I learned was sobering. Market forces don't just respond to inequities. They can perpetuate them.

Now, as a Research Analyst at the Early Childhood Research Alliance of Chicago (EC*REACH), I work with city agencies in Chicago to clean, organize, and analyze administrative data on early childhood education. I led a project to unduplicate children across seven administrative databases using probabilistic matching techniques. I've contributed to several papers evaluating Chicago's Universal Pre-K program. The work is technical, but the stakes are not. Behind every record is a child, and behind every policy decision is the question of whether we're giving that child a fair shot.
